The Path Integral: From Classical to Quantum Action

In1942, Richard Feynman submitted his manuscript for his doctoral thesis, and starting my own academic research adventure, it is a humbling exercise, pondering the profound and far-reaching consequences of his work. Taking inspiration from the (in)famous double-slit experience, and the canonical commutation relation, he reimagined the concept of the classical action principle, the lynchpin of Lagrangian mechanics, to include the quantum realm.

In the classical interpretation of the dynamics of bodies, we use the principle of stationary action to help us determine their equations of motion. In a sense, we determine the trajectory that minimises some quantity that we call the "Action". We emphasise here the singularity of this trajectory, we assume that there is a unique, minimising trajectory. In Feynman's formulation, we operate with a conceptually different principle: bodies with dimensions putting them in the realm of quantum mechanics can take any one of an infinity of potential trajcectories, and each of these contribute, either negatively or positively, with a weighting related to the probability of that trajectory having been taken. In this way our action integral becomes an infinite-dimensional one and we are no longer concerned with the exact trajectory taken, but a quantity known as the transition amplitude, which can be seen as a proxy for the probability that some trajectory was indeed taken.

In the following, I will attempt to motivate a somewhat approachable justification of this approach, and to demonstrate some of the interesting connections that we can draw between quantum mechanics and random processes. One of the most interesting results one ecnounters is the power of the Wick rotation, that is the transformation of the time dimension into an imaginary one, $t \rightarrow -it$, to transform the famous Shcroedinger equation to the diffusion equation. In the absence of a potential, we would have:


$$ \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \psi (q, t) = \frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla ^2 \psi (q,t) \rightarrow i \hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\psi(q,t)=\frac{-\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2 \psi (q,t) $$
